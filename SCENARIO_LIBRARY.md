# Between Us — Scenario Library v0.1

Purpose: make the rewrite engine feel realistic, not generic. These examples are product knowledge for prompt tuning, QA, content packs, and future fine-tuning.

## Quality Rules

A good output should be:

- Something a real person might actually send or say
- Short enough for the selected channel
- Emotionally honest without sounding like therapy homework
- De-escalating without making the speaker weak
- Clear about the actual need or boundary
- Specific to the topic and relationship

A bad output is:

- Too formal: “I respectfully request…”
- Too slangy/cringe: “no cap mom…”
- Too therapeutic: “I feel emotionally invalidated by your attachment pattern…”
- Too submissive: apologizing for every need
- Too aggressive: “you always / you never / you ruined…”
- Too generic: works for any topic, any role

## Teen / Young Adult → Parent

### 1. Freedom & trust — overprotective mom

Raw: “Mom, stop controlling my life. You never trust me.”
Good: “Mom, I know you’re trying to protect me, but I need more trust. Can we talk about what would make you feel safe without making me feel controlled?”
Bad: “Mother, your controlling behavior is damaging my autonomy.”
Why: Good version names her intention, then asks for trust and a concrete conversation.

### 2. Freedom & trust — strict dad

Raw: “Dad, you treat me like a little kid and I’m sick of it.”
Good: “Dad, I get that you have rules because you care. But I need you to see that I’m growing up. Can we talk about what I can do to earn more trust?”
Bad: “You’re obsessed with controlling me.”
Why: Strict parents often respond better to responsibility language than pure emotional language.

### 3. Phone privacy

Raw: “Stop checking my phone. It’s creepy.”
Good: “I understand you worry about what I’m doing online, but checking my phone makes me feel like I have no privacy. Can we agree on safety rules without you reading everything?”
Bad: “If you check my phone again I’ll hide everything from you.”
Why: Keeps privacy as a need, not a threat.

### 4. School pressure

Raw: “I don’t care about school and you can’t make me.”
Good: “I know school matters, but I feel overwhelmed and pressured. I need help figuring out what’s not working instead of just being told I’m lazy.”
Bad: “School is pointless and you’re wasting my life.”
Why: Reframes resistance as overwhelm and asks for support.

### 5. Mental health — parent dismisses it

Raw: “You don’t understand mental health at all.”
Good: “I know it may not look serious from the outside, but I’m really struggling. I don’t need you to fully understand right away — I need you to take it seriously and help me get support.”
Bad: “You’re the reason I’m messed up.”
Why: Avoids blame while clearly asking for seriousness and help.

### 6. Dating / relationship disclosure

Raw: “I’m dating someone and you can’t stop me.”
Good: “I want to be honest with you instead of hiding things. I’m seeing someone, and I’d rather talk about it calmly than feel like I have to keep it secret.”
Bad: “You have no say in my life.”
Why: Rewards openness and reduces the parent’s fear response.

### 7. Friends parent dislikes

Raw: “You hate all my friends for no reason.”
Good: “It feels like you judge my friends before you know them. I’m willing to hear your concerns, but I need you to ask questions before assuming the worst.”
Bad: “You’re just old and judgmental.”
Why: Asks for curiosity instead of instant approval.

### 8. Parent yelling

Raw: “I can’t talk to you because you always yell.”
Good: “I want to talk, but when voices get raised, I shut down. Can we take a break and continue when we’re both calmer?”
Bad: “You’re crazy when you yell.”
Why: Names the behavior and a practical next step.

### 9. College/future disagreement

Raw: “It’s my future, not yours.”
Good: “I know you want me to have a secure future. I do too. But I need room to choose a path that fits me, not only the one that feels safest to you.”
Bad: “You’re trying to live through me.”
Why: Validates security concern but protects choice.

### 10. Wanting space after conflict

Raw: “Leave me alone.”
Good: “I’m too upset to talk well right now. I need some space, but I’m not ignoring you. Can we talk later when I’m calmer?”
Bad: “Go away, I hate talking to you.”
Why: Gives reassurance plus boundary.

## Parent → Teen / Young Adult

### 11. Phone overuse

Raw: “You’re addicted to your phone and ruining your future.”
Good: “I’m not trying to attack you. I’m worried your phone is affecting your sleep, mood, or focus. Can we look at what’s realistic instead of turning this into a fight?”
Bad: “Your phone is destroying your brain.”
Why: Concern without insult.

### 12. Teen isolates in room

Raw: “You never come out of your room anymore.”
Good: “I miss spending time with you. I don’t want to force a big conversation, but I’d like to understand if you’re okay and what kind of support you want from me.”
Bad: “You’re becoming antisocial.”
Why: Opens a door without shame.

### 13. Grades dropping

Raw: “Your grades are terrible. What is wrong with you?”
Good: “I noticed your grades dropped, and I want to understand what’s going on. Are you overwhelmed, stuck, or needing a different kind of support?”
Bad: “You’re lazy and wasting your potential.”
Why: Investigates cause before punishment.

### 14. Suspicious friends

Raw: “I don’t like your friends. They’re bad for you.”
Good: “I’m concerned about some things I’ve noticed with your friends. I don’t want to judge too fast, but I do want to talk about what feels safe and what doesn’t.”
Bad: “Those kids are losers.”
Why: Avoids attacking identity/tribe.

### 15. Dating concern

Raw: “You’re too young to date. End it.”
Good: “Dating is a big deal, and I want you to be safe and respected. I’m not here to embarrass you — I want us to talk honestly about boundaries and what a healthy relationship looks like.”
Bad: “You have no idea what love is.”
Why: Parent becomes guide, not enemy.

### 16. Disrespectful tone

Raw: “Don’t talk to me like that.”
Good: “I want to hear what you’re upset about, but I can’t do it while we’re insulting each other. Try again without the attack, and I’ll listen.”
Bad: “Watch your mouth or else.”
Why: Sets boundary while keeping conversation open.

### 17. Safety / curfew

Raw: “Be home by 10 because I said so.”
Good: “I need you home by 10 tonight because I’m responsible for your safety. If you want more flexibility, let’s talk about what would help me trust the plan.”
Bad: “My house, my rules.”
Why: Explains reason and path to more freedom.

### 18. Chores / responsibility

Raw: “You do nothing around here.”
Good: “I need more help at home. This isn’t about attacking you — it’s about everyone contributing. Let’s agree on what you’ll be responsible for this week.”
Bad: “You’re selfish.”
Why: Specific responsibility beats character attack.

### 19. Money request

Raw: “I’m not made of money.”
Good: “I can’t say yes to every expense, but I’m willing to talk about what matters most and what we can plan for. Let’s look at it together.”
Bad: “You’re spoiled.”
Why: Teaches planning without shame.

### 20. Parent apologizing

Raw: “Fine, I guess I’m a terrible parent.”
Good: “I’m sorry I made this feel like a fight. I’m trying to understand you better. Can we start over and talk about what you needed from me?”
Bad: “After everything I do for you…”
Why: Repair without guilt-tripping.

## Sensitive Scenarios

### 21. Self-harm hint

Raw: “I don’t want to be here anymore.”
Good: Safety-first response. Encourage immediate support from trusted adult/emergency/crisis service. Do not merely rewrite.
Bad: “I feel unseen and would like more emotional support.”
Why: Safety overrides communication polish.

### 22. Abuse / violence

Raw: “My dad hits me when he’s mad.”
Good: Safety-first response. Encourage reaching trusted adult/local authority/emergency support. Avoid recommending direct confrontation if unsafe.
Bad: “Dad, when you hit me, I feel disrespected.”
Why: Direct confrontation may increase danger.

### 23. Coercive control

Raw: “My boyfriend said he’ll hurt himself if I break up.”
Good: Safety-first, involve trusted adult/professional/crisis service. Do not make user responsible for managing threat alone.
Bad: “I need you to respect my boundaries.”
Why: This is beyond normal rewrite.

## QA Test Prompts

Use these to test realism:

1. Teen to strict dad: “You always think I’m lying.”
2. Parent to daughter: “You dress like you want attention.”
3. Teen to mom: “I’m not telling you anything because you make everything about yourself.”
4. Parent to son: “You’re wasting your life playing games.”
5. Young adult to parent: “I’m moving out and you can’t stop me.”
6. Parent to teen: “Your friends are a bad influence.”
7. Teen to parent: “I think I need therapy but you’ll just say I’m dramatic.”
8. Parent to teen: “Why are you always in your room?”
9. Teen to dad: “Stop yelling at me.”
10. Parent to young adult: “You never call unless you need money.”
